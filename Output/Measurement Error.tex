By using a unique Cassava dataset, we demonstrate that there are non-classic measurement errors in self-reported agricultural data, which is endogenous to gender. We analytically discuss the bias in gender analysis with nonlinear models due to the non-classic measurement error. Corrections to the endogenous measurement error is also discussed.

	\section{Introduction} \label{sec:introduction}
	
		The significance of incorporating a gender perspective in various social and economic sectors has been emphasized by policymakers and development professionals since the 1990s. The World Bank, FAO, and IFAD's series of publications (e.g. \cite{quisumbing2014gender, fortmann2009gender}) highlighted that neglecting to acknowledge the varying roles, disparities, and inequalities between men and women in the agricultural sector poses a significant risk to the success of agricultural development efforts

	  Several studies have revealed gender gaps in the adoption of new technologies, as well as non-classical measurement errors in agricultural survey data. On the other hand, recent studies showed that self-reported data is possibly misclassified \citep{wossen2019estimating}. According to \citep{meyer2009under}, survey data on participation in social programs is subject to significant misreporting, with levels of misreporting that can reach as high as 50\%.\cite{wossen2019estimating} indicated that the misreporting of adoption status is not exogenous to the household's characteristics. Furthermore, this misreporting leads to a substantial bias of approximately 22 percentage points in the productivity impact of adoption.
	  
	Although prior research has explored gender gaps in technology adoption, no studies have assessed the influence of non-classical measurement errors on such analyses. Thus, our study aims to fill this gap by examining the potential impact of non-classical errors in agricultural survey data on the estimation of gender gaps in technology adoption. More specifically, this study explicitly demonstrate the measurement errors and apply a household survey data to demonstrate the non-classic error. \textbf{This is the first study that using responses reported by both genders in each household to identify the non-classic measurement error related to gender.  }

	"Cassava (Manihot esculenta), a major staple crop, is the main source of calories for 500 million people across the globe. It can be grown with minimally purchased inputs due to its tolerance to environmental stresses, in particular drought (EL-Sharkawy, 2003). This minimal care, along with being an energy-dense food, make cassava central to food security and resilience strategies (Montagnac, Davis, and Tanumihardjo 2009). The majority of the countries with the highest daily rates of per capita cassava consumption are in Africa, with Nigeria being the world’s largest producer (FAOSTAT n.d.). In Nigeria, cassava is predominantly cultivated by men and women small holders (1-5 hectares) (Joyce Ahmadu and P. O. Idisi 2014). It is also the most important source of carbohydrates after maize and rice, and plays a principal role in the food economy (Udensi E Udensi et al. 2011). Given its importance, major efforts aim to improve cassava through global, regional and national crop improvement programs, leading to new cassava varieties released for adoption (Tahirou Abdoulaye et al. 2014)."
	
	The analysis here draws from a subset of data compiled through the Cassava Monitoring Survey in Nigeria conducted, reviewed for ethics regarding human subject participation (IRB) and published by the International Institute of Tropical Agriculture (IITA) (Wossen et al., 2017). The 16 states that contributed at least 80\% of the total production of cassava comprise the enumeration areas (EA) across four regions (Southwest, Southeast, South-South, and North) were obtained from the National Population Commission of Nigeria. Sample frames of all cassava growing households were developed through agricultural development programs in the targeted states, from which at least 50 cassava growing households were drawn per state for sample size of 125 EAs. In each EA, five household heads and two spouses were interviewed for a total of 625 households in each region. In sum, interviews were conducted with 2500 randomly selected cassava growing households in the major cassava growing states in the four geopolitical regions of Nigeria that together account for over 80% of the annual total cassava production. 
	From this total survey, interviews were conducted with both the identified head of household and an additional member of the household to create a subset of 758 households. From this subset, only those households where both respondents met the following criteria are included in this analysis: the recorded relationship to the household head was self or spouse; status was married; and, both members reported living with spouse. This eliminated 29 households for a sample of 729 households ($1,458$ men and women). Also note that we use the terms husband and wife intentionally, and refrain from using the terms household head and spouse. In this analysis, 13 household heads are women, and 3 spouses are men. Among these responses, the wife of one household responded as the household head and the husband responded as the spouse. For the others, both respondents in 12 households reported being the household head, and both participants from two households reported being the spouse. 	\section{Methodology} \label{sec:methodology}
\textbf{	"Much less is known about the case in which the regressor and the measurement error are both endogenous."} \cite{nguimkeu2019estimation}
	
	Measurement error in a regression analysis refers to the extent to which the measured values of the independent or dependent variables are different from their true values. Non-classical measurement errors occur when the measurement error is not independent of the true value of the variable, or when the measurement error is correlated with the error term in the regression model. This can happen, for example, if the measurement device used is biased, or if there are errors in the data collection process.
	
	Non-classical measurement error can result in biased estimates of the regression coefficients, and can also lead to problems with hypothesis testing. Specifically, non-classical measurement error can lead to attenuation bias, which occurs when the true value of the coefficient is underestimated. This happens because the measurement error causes the observed values of the independent variable to be less correlated with the dependent variable than they actually are. As a result, the regression coefficient is underestimated and the standard errors are inflated, leading to decreased statistical power and potentially false conclusions.
	
	Another problem with non-classical measurement error is that it can lead to omitted variable bias. This occurs when there is a variable that is both correlated with the independent variable and the dependent variable, but is not included in the regression model.
